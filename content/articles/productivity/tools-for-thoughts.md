So far we've discussed how best to [capture]({{< ref "/never-forget-anything.md" >}}) and [arrange]({{< ref "/networked-notes.md" >}}) knowledge: these are lessons you've learned, or information you think is important. I still advocate the approach I described in those articles, at least if you feel that you think in a similar way to me.

What this fails to capture is how to record or work through your ideas about your plans or opinions. I'm going to argue that a perfect system for these latter things may be unattainable. This goes beyond the easiest observations about paralysis by analysis, i.e. that since it's easier to think about doing things than to actually do them, there will always be the temptation to procrastinate and rethink the system instead of using it.

## Thought Taxonomy

I think that thoughts in one's mind occur at different levels of abstraction. This might be due to the complexity of the topic in question, or the degree to which it has "resolved" in your head, or any number of other reasons. Tools for planning stuff, or keeping track of things, work only for thoughts at a certain level of abstraction.

To me, it seems like thoughts occur corresponding roughly the following levels, from most to least abstract:

1. Non-verbal notions or feelings - *these cannot really be recorded: they need more time to resolve*
2. Visual or relational ideas - *these can be recorded and thought through using hand-drawn diagrams and scraps of text*
3. Well-formed ideas or opinions - *these can be written about in long form, like this one*
4. Structured plans for doing things - *these could go into a project management tool, or maybe a well-structured document*
5. Concrete tasks - *these can go into a to-do list system*

I don't mean to imply that every thought can be translated into a plan or a collection of actions - that clearly isn't the case. Not every thought can progress to the end of the list of taxons.

An observation I have made is that the position of a thought on this list cannot reliably be known in advance. If you have a vague impression that you'd like to create an app to do something or other, it feels like you have to write it down right away. When you get to the paper, or your favourite to-do manager, you realize you don't know what to write, because that medium does not match the level of development of the thought. What I find so interesting about this moment - which I have experienced often - is that it comes as a surprise. In my head, it felt like I knew just what to say.

Usually this only goes one way: ideas are further up the list than you think they are.

## Paper vs Computer

At first glance - perhaps because of the examples I've picked - it seems like computers are for thoughts low down the list and paper is for thoughts higher up. I used to think this way for a while, but I now think it's not quite so simple: you can use either for any, as long as the format is correct.

For example, a well-developed \#3 thought would be poorly served by drawing a picture in Paint, but it would also be poorly served by drawing diagrams on paper. The correct *format* for this sort of thought is long-form writing, whether that's in Microsoft Word, VS Code or on paper.

However, I do think the distinction matters in other ways. It has been shown that people who hand-write their notes remember more of them, and anecdotal experience would seem to confirm the "thinking" value of writing by hand. Note that I am not saying the knowledge base approach to notes should be abandoned, because the advantages of using a computer are so great for that practice.

Paper as a rule allows you to draw more quickly and write more slowly than on a computer, but at the same time it makes it much harder to edit what you've already done. Ink can't easily be "deleted", whereas bytes can. We might draw the conclusion that **digital is better for organizing, but analog is better for generating**.

## No System is Perfect

If you, like me, sometimes find yourself on the quest for the single perfect way to organize yourself, you will probably be disappointed forever. The sad truth is that the requirements of such a system will change as *you* change over your life, and since the taxonomy of a thought can't be known in advance, the only way to make sure that you always have a way to think things through is to have *several* media for recording and working through thoughts and plans.

## My Recommendation

If a perfect system is not attainable because its requirements are fluid with respect to the things you use it for, the obvious solution to me is to use as unopinionated a system as possible. What I mean by that is that tools like to-do lists or project management systems constrain you to thinking about the project in a particular way.

Sometimes this is appropriate: if you're working on a software project with a few other people, Trello or Jira are probably good bets for managing your tickets. But that project would be very well-developed, and firmly into level \#5.

Just as the rational solution for knowledge notes is to optimize for ease of access before everything else - because knowledge notes that you have forgotten or can't access may as well not exist - the rational solution for planning things is to optimize for flexibility, since without flexibility you forfeit the ability to develop any thought that lives at the wrong level

While an analog task management system like Bullet Journalling, for example, may be more laborious and less efficient than e.g. Todoist (I like both of these systems very much), it makes that tradeoff willingly for flexibility and closeness to the way you think.

On balance I prefer paper, except where software is obviously the solution, because your mind's relationship with physical objects is different to its relationship with digital ones. It's so easy to type a bunch of things you'd like to do one day into a software tool and then file them away forever - out of sight, out of mind. When they sit in physical ink in a journal on your desk, I honestly think you're more likely to actually do them.
